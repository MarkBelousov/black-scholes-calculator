Black-Scholes Model Guide

What is the Black-Scholes Model?

The Black-Scholes model, also known as the Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) model, is a mathematical model for pricing options contracts. Developed by Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton in the early 1970s, it earned them the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1997.

The model calculates the theoretical value of European-style options using current stock prices, expected dividends, the option's strike price, expected interest rates, time to expiration, and expected volatility.

The Formula

The Black-Scholes formula for European options:

Call: C = S·N(d₁) - K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂)

Put: P = K·e^(-rT)·N(-d₂) - S·N(-d₁)

Where:

d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)T] / σ√T

d₂ = d₁ - σ√T

N(x) represents the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution.

Input Parameters

S - Spot Price

The current market price of the underlying asset (stock, commodity, etc.)

K - Strike Price

The predetermined price at which the option can be exercised

T - Time to Expiry

The time remaining until the option expires, expressed in years

r - Risk-Free Rate

The theoretical return on a risk-free investment, typically based on Treasury yields

σ - Volatility

The annualized standard deviation of the underlying asset's returns

The Greeks Explained

The Greeks are sensitivity measures that describe how an option's price reacts to changes in different variables. Each is named after a Greek letter.

Delta (Δ)

Measures: Price sensitivity to $1 move in underlying

Call Range: 0 to 1.0

Put Range: -1.0 to 0

Meaning: How much the option price changes for each $1 movement in the stock price.

Gamma (Γ)

Measures: Rate of change of Delta

Range: Always positive

Meaning: How fast Delta changes. High Gamma means Delta is very sensitive to price moves.

Theta (Θ)

Measures: Daily time decay

Typical Sign: Negative (options lose value over time)

Meaning: How much value the option loses each day as expiration approaches.

Vega (ν)

Measures: Sensitivity to 1% volatility change

Range: Positive for both calls and puts

Meaning: How much the option price changes for each 1% change in implied volatility.

Rho (ρ)

Measures: Sensitivity to 1% interest rate change

Call Sign: Positive

Put Sign: Negative

Meaning: How much the option price changes for each 1% change in interest rates.

Implied Volatility (IV)

Implied volatility is the market's forward-looking expectation of how much a stock's price will fluctuate. Unlike historical volatility (which looks at past price movements), implied volatility is derived from the option's current market price.

How it's calculated: Using the Black-Scholes model, we input all known variables (S, K, T, r) plus the current market price of the option, then solve for the volatility (σ) that would produce that price.

High IV: Market expects large price swings → Higher option premiums

Low IV: Market expects stability → Lower option premiums

Important Assumptions & Limitations

The Black-Scholes model makes several key assumptions:

  • No dividends are paid during the option's life
  • Markets are efficient (no arbitrage opportunities)
  • No transaction costs or taxes
  • Risk-free rate and volatility are constant
  • Returns are normally distributed (lognormal)
  • Only applies to European options (can only exercise at expiration)

Note: Real markets often violate these assumptions, which is why implied volatility varies across strike prices (volatility smile/skew).

About Black-Scholes Model

The Black-Scholes model is a mathematical model for pricing an options contract. It calculates the theoretical value of European-style options using:

C = S·N(d₁) - K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂)

P = K·e^(-rT)·N(-d₂) - S·N(-d₁)

Where d₁ and d₂ are calculated based on spot price, strike price, time to expiry, risk-free rate, and volatility.
